A

B

C

D

Correct Option
A

Source
Chemistry • solutions
Explanation

Step 1: Formula for Van't Hoff Factor

The Van't Hoff factor ($$i$$) tells us how particles behave in a solution. We can find it using this formula: $$i = \frac{\text{Actual molecular weight}}{\text{Calculated molecular weight}}$$.

Step 2: Find Actual Molecular Weight

The real molecular weight of naphthoic acid (C$$_{11}$$H$$_{8}$$O$$_{2}$$) is 172 g/mol.

Step 3: Find Calculated Molecular Weight

We use the formula:
$$\text{Calculated molecular weight} = \frac{1000 \times w \times K_f}{W \times \Delta T_f}$$

Here,

$$\Delta T_f$$ = Drop in freezing point = 2 K

$$K_f$$ = Freezing point constant = 1.72 K kg mol$$^{-1}$$

$$w$$ = Mass of naphthoic acid used = 20 g

$$W$$ = Mass of benzene (solvent) = 50 g

Put the values in:

$$=\frac{1000 \times 1.72 \times 20}{50 \times 2} = 344$$ g/mol

The calculated molecular weight is 344 g/mol.

Step 4: Calculate Van't Hoff Factor

Now, use the formula from Step 1:

$$i = \frac{\text{Actual molecular weight}}{\text{Calculated molecular weight}}$$

$$i = \frac{172}{344} = 0.5$$

✅ True.

This is due to the paraxial approximation—the mirror formula $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{u} + \frac{1}{v}$ holds only when the mirror aperture is small so that rays make small angles with the principal axis. This avoids spherical aberration.

Laws of reflection are strictly valid for plane surfaces, but not for large spherical surfaces.

❌ False.

The laws of reflection (angle of incidence = angle of reflection) are fundamental and apply to all reflecting surfaces—plane, spherical, or irregular. What changes in large spherical mirrors is that reflected rays do not all converge at a single focus due to geometry, but the law of reflection itself still holds locally at every point on the surface.
